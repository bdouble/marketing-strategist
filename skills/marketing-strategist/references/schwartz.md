# Eugene Schwartz Frameworks

## 5 Levels of Market Awareness

"The more aware your market, the easier the selling job, the less you need to say." Running a "most aware" ad to an "unaware" audience is one of the most costly mistakes in advertising.

### Level 1: Unaware
They do not know they have a problem or that a solution category exists.
- **Copy approach:** Focus on an emotion, attitude, or universal experience. Do not mention the product, problem, or solution. Enter the conversation already in their head.
- **Headline approach:** Evoke emotion or universal experience, then connect to the underlying problem.
- **Formula:** "The [surprising/hidden] reason you're experiencing [symptom they recognize]"
- Most expensive traffic to convert. Requires the most copy.

### Level 2: Problem Aware
They know they have a problem but do not know solutions exist.
- **Copy approach:** Validate the frustration deeply. Show you understand the problem better than they do. Then reveal a solution category exists -- do not reveal the product yet.
- **Headline approach:** Name the problem directly and empathetically, hint at resolution.
- **Formula:** "How to finally [overcome/solve] [specific problem they've been living with]"

### Level 3: Solution Aware
They know solutions exist but do not know about your specific product.
- **Copy approach:** Position as the superior solution. Compare and contrast approaches. Explain why other solutions fail and yours works differently.
- **Headline approach:** Three-component structure: define the need, demonstrate the possibility, show how your product fulfills it.
- **Formula:** "The [specific type] approach to [solution] that actually works -- and why [common approach] keeps failing"

### Level 4: Product Aware
They know your product but are not convinced to buy.
- **Copy approach:** Focus on reinforcing need, emphasizing benefits, proving effectiveness, clarifying how it works, or addressing specific objections. Testimonials, case studies, and demonstrations are primary tools.
- **Headline approach:** Lead with specific result, timeframe, or proof.
- **Formula:** "[Specific result] in [timeframe] using [your method/product]"

### Level 5: Most Aware
They know the product, know they want it, and need a reason to act now.
- **Copy approach:** Remove friction and create urgency. Limited-time offers, bonuses, guarantees, social proof. The pitch can be minimal.
- **Headline approach:** Lead directly with the offer, price, or compelling reason to act today.
- **Formula:** "Get [product] + [bonus] for $[price] -- only [X] spots available"

## 5 Stages of Market Sophistication

Market sophistication = "How many similar products have they been told about before?" Different from awareness levels. Tracks cumulative market exposure to advertising claims.

### Stage 1: First to Market
Market has never heard claims like yours.
- **Strategy:** Make the direct, simple, powerful claim. Do not be clever or indirect.
- **Headline:** State the benefit directly and powerfully. "Lose 30 pounds in 30 days."

### Stage 2: Competition Emerges
Others have entered with similar claims. Direct claims still work but need amplification.
- **Strategy:** Enlarge and extend the claim. Make it bigger, more specific, more dramatic.
- **Headline:** Amplify the claim with specificity and scale.

### Stage 3: Crowded Market
Market has heard every variation of the claim. Claims alone no longer convert.
- **Strategy:** Shift from the claim to the mechanism. The "how" becomes the headline.
- **Headline:** Lead with the unique mechanism: "[The specific mechanism] that [delivers result]"
- Example: "The 3-phase metabolic reset that eliminates fat without calorie restriction"

### Stage 4: Mechanism Saturation
Competitors have copied the mechanism.
- **Strategy:** Enlarge and amplify the mechanism. Make it newer, faster, stronger.
- **Headline:** "[Our mechanism] works 3x faster than [the standard approach]"

### Stage 5: Full Saturation / Identity Market
Nobody believes claims or mechanisms anymore.
- **Strategy:** Abandon claims and mechanisms. Sell through identity. Tribes, movements, values.
- **Headline:** "For people who [identity statement]."

**Running Stage 1 copy into a Stage 5 market is fatal.** Bold claims just confirm you are out of touch.

## Mass Desire and Channeling

"Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product."

### The Three-Part Channeling Process
1. Identify the pre-existing mass desire in your market (research, not assumption)
2. Find the language and images that unlock and amplify that desire
3. Connect that desire specifically and exclusively to your product

Market research is not optional -- it is the entire job. Finding out what people already desperately want, in their own words, is the first and most important task. Then reflect it back, focused on your solution.

## 13 Desire Amplification Techniques

| # | Technique | Application |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Direct presentation | Describe the product and its satisfaction in complete detail |
| 2 | Put claims in action | Show exactly how it works and delivers |
| 3 | Bring in the reader | Make the prospect the protagonist of the experience |
| 4 | Show how to test claims | Let readers visualize proving the product works |
| 5 | Stretch benefits over time | Show improvements continue daily, weekly, monthly |
| 6 | Bring in an audience | Surveys, statistics, testimonials, success stories |
| 7 | Show experts approving | Authority figures endorsing the product |
| 8 | Compare and prove superiority | Before vs. after. Old way vs. new way |
| 9 | Picture the negative side | Show life without the solution, then present the product as healing |
| 10 | Show ease of getting benefits | Eliminate friction: price, availability, usability |
| 11 | Use metaphor and imagination | Dramatic language representing facts compellingly |
| 12 | Summarize | Bullet-point recaps of benefits in fresh language |
| 13 | Put the guarantee to work | Make the guarantee a climax, not a footnote |

## 7 Meta-Level Techniques

| Technique | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Intensification | Amplify desire using emotionally charged language, sensory detail, and vivid imagery |
| Identification | Align product with consumer's self-image, aspirations, or group identity |
| Gradualization | Build argument step by step, each claim making the next one logical and inevitable |
| Redefinition | Reframe the prospect's objections, redefine what the product means so resistance dissolves |
| Mechanization | Explain how the product works -- the mechanism satisfies the rational mind and builds credibility |
| Concentration | Destroy alternate ways the prospect might satisfy their desire -- disqualify competition and prove only your product delivers what they want |
| Camouflage | Borrow conviction from external credibility -- present the ad as editorial, expert analysis, or educational content so sales resistance is never activated |

## Voice Profile

- Analytical, precise, intellectual
- Framework-first thinking
- Refers to "the prospect" and "the market"
- Builds layered arguments step by step
- Headlines treated as both art and science
- "The desire already exists..." philosophy
- Never creates demand; channels existing demand
- Clinical precision in language
- Deep psychological insight into buyer motivation
- "The copy writer's first job is not to write copy. It is to know the market." philosophy
